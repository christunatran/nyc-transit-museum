### use our existing data to tell a lesser known story about how there used to be multiple train companies that were later absorbed into the MTA
three companies:
- IRT (interborough rapid transit) -- the original, opened 1904. ran lexington ave line, 7th ave line, flushing line, the elevated lines in manhattan and the bronx. private, profitable at first, then squeezed to death by the nickel fare they were contractually locked into while costs rose. filed for bankruptcy in 1932. city bought it in 1940 for $151 million
- BMT (brooklyn-manhattan transit) -- grew out of brooklyn elevated lines, expanded through dual contracts of 1913 into manhattan. ran waht are not N/Q/R/W/, B/D, J/M/Z, L. also went bankrupt. city bought it simultaneously with IRT in 1940 for $175 million
- IND (independent city-owned rapid transit) -- city built this one itself starting in 1932, explicitly to compete with and eventually replace the private operators. it was the only line built with public money from the start. ran the A/C/E, B/D/F/M, G
- in 1932-1939 data can see the three companies as separate line items w/ separate ridership counts.
    - IND starts from 0 in 1932 and grows explosively
    - IRT and BMT flatline or decline
    - private operators are visibly being cannibalized by the public competitor before the city officially buys them out in 1940
- 1940 data is the first year they appera as a unified system
    - transition from three separate numbers to one is the story


## data
- station-level ridership for 460 NYC subway stations, 1940-1995, sourced from archival transit commission 
pdfs obtained from transit museum
- station-level data
    - 1920-1939: station by station, from transit commission reports
    - 1940-1995: station by station, from MTA OMB (office of management and budget)compilation
- system-wide totals only:
    - 1940-2011: annual subway + bus totals
- [ ] pre-1940 data exists (1920-1939) but at a different level of aggregation and with different station naming conventions -- cross-decade matching is only possible for ~28% of stations 
- [ ] the 2012-2026 gap is real and unfilled; MTA open data exists to close it but i haven't pulled it yet
- the annotations file is valuable b/c it gives year-by-year historical context that transforms numbers into narrative

## shape of data
- [ ] system peak: 1946 at 2.1 billion rides
- [ ] system nadir: 1977 at 912 million -- less than half the peak, first time under 1 billion since before WWII
- [ ] most dramatic individual decline: Bowery lost 94% between 1946 and 1977 
- [ ] most dramatic recovery: Utica Ave gained 247% between 1977 and 1995, driven by Caribbean immigrant communities rebuilding Crown Heights
- [ ] W. 4th st barely moved across 50 years -- some stations are eternal anchors
- [ ] Cortlandt st flatlines to 0 in 1982 -- data encodes physical destruction of the station

## strongest editorial moments in the data 
- 1932: the IND opens -- new transit system born from zero
- 1946: the peak -- city at maximum density and mobility
- 1955: 3rd ave El disappears from data entirely
- 1966: the TWU strike -- one year of near-zero on certain lines
- 1968-1977 -- the collapse, line by line, neighborhood by neighborhood
- 1977: the nadir -- # that tells the whole story of 1970s New York
- 1944: MetroCard appears -- the beginning of recovery visible in the numbers
