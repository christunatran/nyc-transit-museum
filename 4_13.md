## new story: the story of how the IRT/BMT/IND got combined, within 1920-1940, as told by data

## what does granular ridership reveal that aggregate doesn't? few candidate stories:
- depression geography -- Depression didn't hit all neighborhoods equally. Manhattan core station hed steady; outer-borough stations collapsed.
- IND cannibalization, station-level
    - when the IND 8th ave opened in 1932, which IRT/BMT stations nearby lost riders? if at all?
- WWII surge localization -- shipyard and defense-plant stations in Brooklyn/Queens spiked; shopping/tourist stations didn't as much
- individual station acrs -- Times Square vs. an abandoned-by-1940 elevated station vs a brand new IND station


### The narrative arc the data actually supports
- 1920-1928: the duopoly era. IRT and BMT, ~1.3-1.9B total rides annually. Growing. Two colors on your chart.
- 1929-1931: the crash. Depression hits. Ridership holds at first (unemployed still look for work), then drops. Both private companies in financial distress.
- 1932: the pivot. IRT enters receivership (August 25). IND opens (September 10). Third color appears on your chart, starting at zero.
- 1933-1939: the slow squeeze. IND grows from 59M to 343M. IRT drops from 1.21B to 906M. BMT drops from 654M to 594M. Total system flat — the new line is eating the old lines. La Guardia pushes for municipal ownership.
- June 1940: unification. City buys BMT ($175M, June 1) and IRT ($151M, June 12). Three colors become one. Your chart's three streams converge into one band. 1.84B rides that year, under one management.

## need to normalize ridership data

### "ridership" meaning, corrected
- pre-1940: fare-paying entries (at turnstile OR ticket office) per Transit Commisson filings, by company
- post-1940: turnstile registrations per Board of Transportation count
- post-1994: turnstile registrations including MetroCard

^^^ all three are "fare events" counted at the entry point

### is it ethical to represent this^^ as ridership
- what we are measuring 1920-1950 is: fare-paying entries by station
    - same as what every official "NYC Subway ridership" number in history-- just using the same definition the MTA still uses today
- ethical footnote: "ridership means fare events, not unique humans, and that's been true since 1904".

### what "ridership" means in the data
`ridership` is counted differently in every era

Summary: **across the whole dataset, `ridership` never means unique riders`. pre-1940 it's fare dollars (at the fixed 5 cent fare). post-1940 it's turnstile counter registrations. post-1994 it's turnstile registrations plus MetroCard entries. Same person, two rides = two counts in all cases

- pre-1940 (private_operators): fares collected in dollars, not unique passengers
 - [ ] don't the fares change across the years and ANNOTATIONS.md covers that?

- post-1940 (board_of_transportation, mta): "registerations" -- turnstile entries counted, not unique passengers

- neither era counts "unique humans who rode."
    - both count "revenue events attributable to a station"
    - when we say "ridership at Times Square in 1925," the honest reading is "fare-paying entries registered at Times Square turnstiles or their accounting equivalent"


### joint-station specifics
- there are 567 pre-1940 joint-operation rows

- question: "how much fare revenue did IRT book from this station after splitting with BMT?"
    - it is not even "IRT's riders" -- an IRT passenger and a BMT passegenger both paid into a common pool, then the pool was split contractually.
